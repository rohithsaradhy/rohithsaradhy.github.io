Why A Career in Physics?

It was a cold summer night at the Institute of Physics(IOP) XRD facility that the stepper motor had finally clicked using the Huber Stepper Motor Controller(SMC). Eavesdropping on the RS-232 communication between the stock software and the Huber SMC confirmed my belief that the Visual Basic application I had written to interface the machine was not at fault. The problem was with the way byte code for carriage return was sent to the machine in a Windows operating system. Having been working on this project for more than 2 months as a second year Int. M.Sc. student I couldn’t wait to tell my summer guide, Prof. P.V Satyam what the problem had been all along. Hearing this, he gave the “go ahead” signal for me to interface the X-Y stepper motor stage in the microbeam line of the accelerator facility at IOP. And I began my expedition to set myself on the stage as an experimental physicist.

My research interests continued to mature, streamlined in the direction of developing experiments and instrumentation to explore the unknown physics. Being selected for Visiting Students Research Programme at the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research under the guidance of Prof. L.C Tribedi gave me the opportunity to work with the ECR Ion Accelerator where I helped develop post-collision charge state deflector. The satisfaction of developing one’s own experimental equipment with the endless possibility of doing exciting new physics drove me further into seeking out such projects.

The coursework at the National Institute of Science Education and Research, where I was admitted as an undergraduate was also a decisive factor in shaping my interests and experience. While the core courses instilled in me a deep understanding of physics, the advanced courses set me apart from the crowd, coaching me in the areas of algorithms, experimental techniques, information theory, high energy physics to name a few. Lab courses played a crucial role in facilitating the grasp of what was taught in the theory courses. I was fascinated by the ingenious methods that were employed to explore the realm of the unknown such as the Michelson Interferometer, Lock-In Amplifier, and Laser-Doppler Anemometer. With the help of Dr Ashok Mohapatra, we developed a photo-diode detector to replace the conventional measuring technique of the Michelson interferometer during one of the lab courses. This exercise, along with the previous projects got me thinking about the essential requirement of a detector in any experiment. And understanding detectors means devising more sensitive experiments with the ultimate aim of exploring the unexplored.

With the aim of understanding and building efficient detectors, I chose the field of gaseous detector development for my Master’s thesis under the guidance of Prof. Bedangadas Mohanty. Working with Bakelite RPC and studying charged particle interaction with matter along with the simulation of the detector geometry using Geant4 has introduced me to the parameters that characterise an efficient detector. Furthermore, I plan to extend this project to include the study of neutral particle interactions with matter, develop and test proportional counters and use Geant4 to simulate the Germanium detectors used in SuperCDMS experiment at SNOLab to see how fractional charges interact with matter.

My immediate educational objectives are to acquire the expertise to build efficient, high-resolution detectors and implement them in ongoing experiments to explore physics beyond the standard model. And later set myself up as an experimental researcher, studying and devising experiments to solve the problems that we have no solution for such as the matter-antimatter asymmetry, dark matter and dark energy to name a few.

Beam Optimization and Development of Post Collision Charge State Deflector for ECR Ion Accelerator

Electron Cyclotron Resonance Ion Accelerator (ECRIA) is well known to produce low energy (keV), highly charged ions. The energetic ions from the ECRIA can be made to collide with target atoms or molecules of our interest and by analysing different reaction products we can extract information about the involved reaction mechanism. Recoil Ion Momentum Spectroscopy is one of the techniques to study the momentum distributions of the post collision recoil ions. A complete RIMS system needs a charge state analyser to determine the final charge state after collisions. In keV projectile energy range electron capture to the projectile is one of the dominant mechanisms. To separate out this channel from other possible reaction channels, the post collision projectile charge state analysis is necessary. To this end, I was involved in developing a Post Collision Charge State Deflector for the ECR Ion Accelerator. The project introduced me to whole new horizon of developmental aspect. Design and simulating an entire chamber which was to be mounted as a part of the ongoing experiment. Gave me insight into how the dynamics of a world class research group functions as well as how to work as a team. Learnt about different types of detection methods used and accelerator physics as well as the sophisticated electronics that accompanied with it. Here Solidworks was used to develop the chamber and flanges (Learning a lot about the different types of flanges and sealing technologies). I also learnt about different vacuum technologies that were used in accelerators. During the stay in TIFR I was invited to visit many labs as part of the VSRP programme, which broadened my idea of the research front. I was also awarded the BEST VSRP PROJECT AWARD 2015 for the same.

Under the guidance of Prof. L.C Tribedi at TIFR, Mumbai

Project Report

Development of Photodiode Detector to Study Magnetostriction using Michelson Interferometry

During the 8th semester of the Int. M.Sc. we were encouraged to pursue open ended experiments as a part of the teaching labs. Along with my colleage, we developed a photodiode detector to detect the fringe variations of the Michelson Interferometer in order to study the magnetostriction property of different materials. During the development of this detector the main problem we faced was that the noise due to the environmental vibrations drowned our signal and was solved by using a Low Pass Filter with a very low cut-off frequency. We implemented a stepper motor controlled by an arduino to vary the voltage so that our signal of interest would be of very low frequency too and won't be cut-off by the LPF. Finally we were able to get really good results over the measurements without the detector. I also proposed a model to take into account the thermal expansion effects as well, due to the heating of the solenoid. This was the experiment that we implemented all the things we learned before and successfully achieved the objectives which boosted my confidence in doing more development oriented work.

Under the guidance of Dr. Ashok Mohapatra, NISER, Bhubaneswar.
Built Photodector, Overcame Enviromental Noise, Stepper Motor Controlled Voltage Variation, Proposed a Model for Temperature Effects
